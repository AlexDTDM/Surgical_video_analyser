Project specification

Operative reports from surgical video

Turn endoscopic video into a structured draft operative report a surgeon reviews, completes, and signs. Video is the only required input.

Vision-only Draft + sign-off First procedure: lap. chole
01

Summary

A system that ingests endoscopic video from a surgical procedure (robotic or laparoscopic) and produces a structured draft operative report describing what was done and what was found. The video is the only required input. The output is a draft that a surgeon reviews, completes, and signs — not an autonomous final document.

The technical core is a two-step idea: first recognize what is happening in the video as a stream of discrete, time-stamped events (surgical phases and action triplets); then turn that structured event stream into operative-report prose.

02

Problem and scope

Surgeons spend meaningful time dictating or writing operative notes after each case. Much of the content of those notes describes observable intraoperative activity — the steps performed, instruments used, structures handled, and findings — which is exactly what a model watching the video could reconstruct. Automating the draft reduces documentation burden while keeping the surgeon as the accountable author.

In scope · v1

The technique and findings narrative — the parts of the report that are visible in the operative field.

Out of scope · v1

Fields not derivable from video: identifiers, indication, consent, blood loss, specimen disposition, instrument/sponge counts. Left as templated placeholders for the surgeon.

03

Goals and non-goals

Goals

  • Generate a faithful, structured draft of the technique/findings sections from video alone.
  • Be vendor- and modality-agnostic: run on any endoscopic video, robotic or laparoscopic, any camera.
  • Keep every stage inspectable so errors can be traced to a specific component.
  • Keep the surgeon in the loop as the final author.

Non-goals

  • No autonomous, unsupervised report finalization.
  • No dependency on proprietary robot telemetry (e.g. da Vinci kinematic logs) in v1. Kinematics is a later accuracy upgrade, not a requirement.
  • No claim to fill fields that are not observable in the video.
04

Key design decisions

  • Vision-first. Runs on video, which generalizes across robots, vendors, and laparoscopic cases, and avoids dependence on vendor-gated kinematic streams.
  • Draft, not final. The deliverable requires surgeon sign-off — both a safety requirement (hallucinated operative detail is a real harm) and a regulatory simplification.
  • One procedure first. Build and validate end-to-end on a single data-rich procedure (laparoscopic cholecystectomy) before generalizing. Robotic prostatectomy second.
  • Structured intermediate representation. A machine-readable event timeline sits between perception and language — the contract between the two halves, the thing you validate, and the thing a surgeon can audit.

5.2 — The data that does not exist publicly

Paired (video → real operative report) data is the true bottleneck. Public datasets give labels for perception but not the target narrative. The acquisition strategy doubles as customer development: partner with one or two surgeons or a single department who already record endoscope video and write op notes per case. A few hundred cases of one procedure, de-identified and paired with the real report, beats any scraped corpus and becomes a defensible data asset.

5.3 — Governance

Surgical video is protected health information. Build for de-identification, IRB/ethics approval, and a signed data-use agreement (BAA) from day one. The public research datasets above carry research-use licenses — fine for prototyping and publication, but verify terms before shipping anything commercial.

06

Core data model

The structured timeline is the heart of the system. Each event is a time-stamped, typed record. The action triplet ⟨instrument, verb, target⟩ is the atomic unit — essentially a sentence of the operative note in structured form.

{
  "t_start": 1423.5,
  "t_end": 1457.2,
  "phase": "calot_triangle_dissection",
  "triplet": {
    "instrument": "hook",
    "verb": "dissect",
    "target": "cystic_artery"
  },
  "confidence": 0.91
}

A procedure becomes an ordered list of such events plus phase boundaries. The report generator consumes this list, not the raw video.

07

Evaluation and success criteria

  • Perception layer. Standard metrics on public benchmarks — phase accuracy and Jaccard, triplet mAP, tool/segmentation scores.
  • Report quality. Automatic text metrics (ROUGE, BERTScore) against held-out real op notes are weak. The primary metric is blinded surgeon review scoring drafts on factual accuracy, completeness, hallucination rate, and edit distance to a sign-off-ready note.
  • Top-line success. A surgeon spends meaningfully less time producing a final note from the draft than writing from scratch, with no factual errors surviving to sign-off.
08

RoadmapEach phase gated by a falsifiable hypothesis

A phase is not done when the work is built — it is done when its hypothesis is confirmed. A refuted hypothesis is a signal to pivot or stop, not to push harder. Each phase tests the assumption that, if false, would invalidate the phases after it.

Phase 0 · Scoping

Lock procedure, template, schema, data & partner

Choose the target procedure (lap. cholecystectomy), report template, and event schema. Secure dataset access and a design-partner surgeon.

HypothesisThe technique/findings content of a real operative note can be expressed as a sequence of observable phases and action triplets — the report is reconstructible from what the camera sees.

TestHave a surgeon map each technique/findings sentence of sample op notes to phase/triplet events; measure the fraction grounded in observable events vs. non-visual knowledge.

KillIf much of the narrative depends on non-visual knowledge, the video-only premise is wrong.

Phase 1 · Perception

Phase & triplet recognition on a frozen backbone

Fine-tune lightweight heads on a frozen surgical video backbone using Cholec80 + CholecT50.

HypothesisA frozen pretrained surgical backbone plus lightweight heads reaches usable phase and triplet accuracy — no pretraining or end-to-end training from scratch needed.

TestBenchmark against published SOTA on Cholec80/CholecT50 with a pre-registered "usable" threshold.

KillIf frozen-backbone probing lands far below threshold, the cheap-transfer assumption fails and cost/scope change materially.

Phase 2 · Timeline

De-noise predictions into an event list

Convert per-frame predictions into a clean, time-stamped event list via smoothing, segment merging, and confidence thresholds.

HypothesisNoisy per-frame predictions can be post-processed into a timeline accurate enough that downstream report errors are dominated by perception, not by timeline construction.

TestCompare constructed timelines against ground-truth annotations (boundary precision/recall) and attribute downstream errors to perception vs. timeline-building.

KillIf timeline construction is a large independent error source, the clean intermediate-representation assumption is weaker than designed.

Phase 3 · Report generation

Constrained LLM: timeline → prose

Build a template-constrained LLM stage mapping the timeline to technique/findings prose, forbidding claims not grounded in events.

HypothesisGiven an accurate timeline, a constrained LLM produces prose surgeons rate as faithful and near sign-off ready, with effectively zero ungrounded claims.

TestFeed ground-truth ("oracle") timelines to the generator and have surgeons rate faithfulness, completeness, and hallucination — isolating the language stage from perception error.

KillIf even oracle input yields hallucinations or unusable prose, generation (not perception) is the blocker.

Phase 4 · Validation

Blinded review of the full pipeline

Blinded surgeon review of the full (non-oracle) pipeline against real op notes.

HypothesisWith the real end-to-end pipeline, a surgeon produces a final note from the draft in meaningfully less time than from scratch, with no factual error surviving to sign-off.

TestTimed within-surgeon comparison (draft-assisted vs. scratch) plus a factual-error audit of signed notes.

KillNo net time saving, or errors reaching sign-off, means the value proposition is unproven.

Phase 5 · Robotic + paired data

Port to SAR-RARP50; start collecting pairs

Port the pipeline to robotic prostatectomy; begin collecting paired video↔report data from the design partner.

Hypothesis A · transferThe architecture ports to a robotic procedure with retraining but no redesign — the approach is procedure-general, not lap-chole-specific.
Hypothesis B · acquisitionDesign-partner surgeons reliably supply de-identified paired video↔report at a usable rate and completeness.

TestReplicate Phase 1–4 metrics on SAR-RARP50; track paired cases per week and pairing completeness over a pilot.

KillA per-procedure redesign is needed (no generality), or the data pipeline yields too few/incomplete pairs to train on.

Phase 6 · Advanced

End-to-end VLM, then kinematics

Explore the end-to-end VLM (Architecture B) once paired data exists; later evaluate adding kinematics as a second modality.

Hypothesis A · VLMGiven the accumulated paired data, an end-to-end VLM beats the modular pipeline on surgeon-rated quality by enough to justify its lower auditability.
Hypothesis B · kinematicsAdding kinematics improves action recognition or report accuracy enough to justify the data-access cost.

TestHead-to-head modular vs. VLM on held-out cases; ablation with/without kinematics where both are available.

KillNo gain over the modular pipeline (stay modular), or no lift from kinematics (don't pay for the access).

Architecture A — Modular pipelineRecommended for v1

Perception and language are separate, connected by the structured timeline. This is the lower-risk, more debuggable path and the recommended starting point.

flowchart TD
    V["Surgical video
(endoscopic frames)"] --> ENC["Surgical video encoder
(frozen foundation model)"]
    ENC --> P["Phase recognition"]
    ENC --> T["Action-triplet recognition
(instrument-verb-target)"]
    ENC --> TL["Tool detection / tracking"]
    ENC --> A["Anatomy segmentation"]
    P --> TS["Structured timeline
(time-stamped events, JSON)"]
    T --> TS
    TL --> TS
    A --> TS
    TS --> LLM["Report generator
(template-constrained LLM)"]
    LLM --> R["Draft operative report"]
    R --> H["Surgeon review + sign-off"]
Fig. A — perception → structured timeline → constrained generation → sign-off

Components

  • Video encoder. A pretrained surgical video foundation model as a frozen feature extractor (e.g. SurgVISTA, SurgeNetXL, EndoFM).
  • Perception heads. Lightweight, separately trainable heads for phase, triplet, tool, and anatomy (attentive probing / adapters on the frozen backbone).
  • Timeline builder. Deterministic post-processing — smoothing, segment merging, confidence gating — producing the event list.
  • Report generator. An LLM prompted with the event list and template, constrained to ground every statement in an event.

Pros

  • Each stage independently testable.
  • Errors traceable to a component.
  • Timeline is clinician-auditable.
  • Lower hallucination risk — describes structured input, not pixels.
  • Smaller data needs; leans on existing labeled sets.

Cons

  • Errors compound across stages.
  • Information outside the schema is lost.
  • More moving parts to build and maintain.
10

Architecture B — End-to-end VLM

A surgical vision-language model maps video directly to report text, with no explicit intermediate representation. This follows recent surgical VLMs (Surgical-LVLM, GP-VLS, Halsted) and the broader "modality-encoder into a frozen LLM" recipe (Flamingo-style cross-attention, as in time-series LMs like OpenTSLM).

flowchart TD
    V["Surgical video
(sampled clips)"] --> ENC["Surgical video encoder"]
    ENC --> ADP["Cross-attention adapter
(Flamingo-style / Q-Former)"]
    ADP --> LLM["Pretrained LLM
(fine-tuned, e.g. LoRA)"]
    PR["Report template + prompt"] --> LLM
    LLM --> R["Draft operative report"]
    R --> H["Surgeon review + sign-off"]
Fig. B — video tokens injected into a fine-tuned LLM via a cross-attention adapter

Components

  • Video encoder. Same surgical backbone family as Architecture A, producing visual tokens.
  • Adapter. A cross-attention module (Flamingo-style) or Q-Former that injects visual tokens into the LLM's attention.
  • Language model. A pretrained LLM fine-tuned (typically LoRA) to emit the report, conditioned on visual tokens and a template-bearing prompt.

Pros

  • No information lost to a fixed schema.
  • Fewer components once trained.
  • Rides advances in general VLMs.

Cons

  • Needs scarce paired video→report data.
  • Harder to validate and constrain; higher hallucination risk.
  • Black box — failures hard to localize or explain.
  • Long procedures stress VLM sequence limits.

Choosing between them

DimensionA — ModularB — End-to-end VLM
Build riskLowerHigher
DebuggabilityHigh (per-stage)Low (black box)
Hallucination controlStrong (event-grounded)Weaker
Data needsExisting labeled setsScarce paired video↔report
Clinical auditabilityHigh (inspectable timeline)Low
Quality ceilingCapped by the schemaPotentially higher
RecommendationBuild Architecture A for v1 to reach a validated, auditable product quickly and start accumulating paired data. Treat Architecture B as a Phase-6 research track that becomes viable once paired data exists — the two share the same video encoder, so the investment carries over. A hybrid is natural: use A's structured timeline as grounding context for a VLM, getting the auditability of A with some expressiveness of B.
12

Key risks

  • Compounding perception errors in Architecture A — mitigated by confidence gating and surgeon review.
  • Hallucinated operative detail — mitigated by event-grounded generation, output constraints, and mandatory sign-off.
  • Paired-data scarcity — mitigated by the design-partner acquisition strategy.
  • Regulatory and privacy exposure — mitigated by de-identification, IRB/BAA, and positioning as documentation support with a human author.
  • Generalization across surgeons, hospitals, and equipment — mitigated by starting narrow and expanding deliberately.
